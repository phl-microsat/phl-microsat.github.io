Project 3 - Development of a Data Processing, Archiving, and Distribution Sub-system for the Ground Receiving Station of the Philippine Microsatellite (PHL-MICROSAT)

Leader: Mark Edwin A. Tupas

Agency/Address: Training Center for Applied Geodesy and Photogrammetry and the Department of Geodetic Engineering, University of the Philippines, Diliman, Quezon City, 1101 PHILIPPINES

Telephone/Fax/Email: +632-920-8924

Significance

Remote Sensing (RS) applications encompasses almost all national government initiatives, these includes applications for Disaster Risk Reduction, Resource Mapping, Food Security and Defense among others. To reap the benefits of RS, operationalization of remote sensing applications and products must be put forward. Operationalization can happen when an automated request and delivery system is in place. The development of data processing, archiving and distribution system would stream line the delivery of RS products to end-users such as NGAs, LGUs and researchers.

Cooperating Agencies:

DOST PCIEERD, DOST ASTI, UP IESM, UP EEEI, Tohoku University, Hokkaido University

Research and Development Station:

Training Center for Applied Geodesy and Photogrammetry

Site of Implementation:

Philippines (nationwide)

Objectives:

The objectives of the project are as follows:

  1. To design and develop data processing, archiving and distribution sub-systems for multiple sensor RS images.
  2. To gain proficiency in the upscaling and automating RS data calibration
  3. To be able to disseminate different RS products (e.g. Land Cover, Resource Maps, etc.)

Review of Related Literature:

In Tohoku University, microsatellites have been developed and deployed with mission instruments that include a 5-m resolution high precision telescope (HPT, 10-cm dia., 1-m focal length) coupled with a similar Liquid Crystal Tunable Filter (650nm - 1050nm) that allows for multispectral earth observation. A ground receiving station in Tohoku University serves as the primary station for data receiving and spacecraft operations. It is supported by U-band uplink and S-band downlink with 2.4-m dia. antenna. Ground stations in Kiruna, Sweden and in Thailand will also be used as data receiving stations.

CRISP Ground Station of the National University of Singapore receives various commercial optical and radar satellite images. Aside from commercial satellite data, CRISP also manages and controls univeristy developed microsatellites. The ground station, which was developed locally, includes subsystems for planning and control. RS data related subsystems also include 1) LAN connected Operations Console, 2) Product Management Subsystem for archiving, 3) Direct Injection Subsystem to receive, calibrate images and a 4) User Interface Subsystem for data distribution.

In GISTDA (Thailand), early designs of a GRS include a GRS Data Processing System that is composed of a Microimage Quick look sub-sytem and a Geocoded Correction Image Subsystem. The Microimage Quick Look sub-system is a pre-processing unit for individual satellites. It calibrates "thumbnails" of datasets, and calculates the percent cloud cover per scene for optical images which are used for previews before actual processing. The Geocoded Image correction subsytem on the other hand generates the designated RS data products.

Another example of a microsatellite initiative is the Science and Technology Satellite-3 (STSAT-3) of Satellite Technology Research Center, Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology. The STSAT-3 system consists of a space segment, ground segment, launch service segment, and various external interfaces. The ground station segment provides the capability to monitor and control STSAT-3, conduct STSAT-3 mission planning, and receive, process, and distribute STSAT-3 payload data. The ground station design for low cost and high efficiency outputs was implemented use of indigenously developed hardware and software subsystems.

Rau et. al. states that most of the commercial packages for data processing are not completely suitable for operational use in massive production. This is because most commercial packages are dedicated to individual pre-defined applications. To solve this concern the Center for Space and Remote Sensing Research of the National Central University of Taiwan, designed an, indigenous, workorder-based multi-sensor geocoded production (MSGP) system in a lan/web based environment.

The major functions of the MSGP system are (1) web querying and ordering, (2) workorder management, (3) orbit modeling and quality control, and (4)ortho-rectification kernel.

Scientific Basis/Theoretical Framework

Ground receiving stations serves multiple purposes, these 1) orbit and attitude control, 2) data acquisition tasking, 3) data receiving, 4) data processing, and 5) data archiving and distribution. While control, tasking and data reception may be satellite specific, data processing and distribution substems are not. Data processing and distribution systems most often than not are extensible to different commercial sensors as well as indigenous satellite payload data.

Data processing subsystems are tasked with converting received raaw satellite data into usable data products. Remote sensing data products varies from raw data, to low level radiometrically and geometrically calibrated images, to higher level application products such as land cover, vegetation indices, etc. While research based user usually prefer lower level products for further processing, operational users such as NGAs would have direct use for higher level products.

For data products to be accessible to end users, an effective data distribution and archiving platform should be in place. Most space based data distribution platforms, are internet based systems capable of taking queries, data request, and download request. The subsystem is envisaged to be a starting point to providing a centralized catalog for RS data in the country.

As noted from various ground receiving stations, indegenous technology for data processing and distribution is important to their success. Usually, commercial vendors offer off the self, if not back box, solutions where generic data products are generated. Local development of such as subsystems would encourage operational use of RS data by churning out specific usable data products.

Methodology:

As needed for the data processing pipe line, the following information and research outputs must be consolidated: 1) calibration procedures for each sensor, 2) DTMs and GPS control points for GCP database (to be used for geometric correction), and 3) Data product generation procedures. The project will leverage existing and established procedures to be able to generate high quality RS data products. The design and developement of the 1) data processing, and 2) archiving and distribution platform would then be initiated with the end users in mind. The developement of the subsystems should allow for easy access and download to all stakeholders. Operations and support to the developed system would be perfomed as a reaserch oppurtunity before a full scale GRS implementation.

Activities to be undertaken: • Identification of satellite sensor data feeds available for ground receiving station e.g. RISESAT, RISING 2 and PHL Microsatellite. • Needs assessment of stakeholders to determine RS data product requirements. • Consolidate calibration procedures for each sensor. • Consolidate available DTMs and GPS control points for GCP database (to be used for geometric correction). • Consolidate data product generation procedures. • Design data processing sub-system. • Design data archiving and data distrbution sub-system. • Subsystem Implementation • Operations and support

Expected Output:

The expected outputs from the project are as follows:

  1. GRS Data processing sub-system
  2. GRS Data archiving sub-system 3. GRS Data distribution sub-system
  3. Access to TBD data products.
