Significance:

This proposed activity will ensure that the instruments onboard the PHL-MICROSAT are properly calibrated and validated so that they become ready for analysis for different applications.

Objectives:

The objectives of the project are as follows:

1. To develop a calibration method to be applied for the remote sensing instrument.
2. To generate a set of calibration parameters for the remote sensing instrument to maintain image spectral fidelity and consistency.
3. To generate an authoritative spectral signature database/library of key ground objects of significance and/or importance that are especially endemic to the Philippine conditions.

Review of Related Literature:

Microsatellites developed and deployed by Tohoku Universiy includes mission instruments that includes a 5-m resolution high precision telescope (HPT, 10-cm dia., 1-m focal length) coupled with a similar Liquid Crystal Tunable Filter (650nm - 1050nm) allows for multispectral earth observation. The aim of radiometric calibration approach that does not rely on sensor-based devices. The goal is to determine the relationship between incident spectral radiance and the sensor output Independent of on-board calibrators (solar diffusers and lamps, for example) and pre-flight

Scientific Basis/Theoretical Framework

Radiance validation is similar but it assumes that the calibration is already known. Preflight or onboard calibration gives the radiometric calibration and thus reported sensor radiance. Independent method predicts radiance at the sensor. If the predicted radiance matches the sensor radiance to within the uncertainties of the two methods, the sensor radiance is validated. Radiance validation is most critical for the accurate retrieval of geophysical parameters from temporal data sets with little to no overlap. Realistically, absolute radiometric calibration may not be needed for some specific cases. Data from a single sensor with focus on change analysis. Multiple sensors for which significant overlap exists. Temporal studies and inter-sensor measurements, however, critically require validated sensors. Biases between sensors need to be removed. Temporal changes in response must be tracked. Temporal studies using multiple sensors with little to no overlap in data MUST have accurate absolute radiometric calibration.

Methodology:

The following activities will be conducted:

  1. Atmospheric characterization. This requires the determination of aerosol amount and the development of aerosol phase function and single scatter albedo. This includes the estimatin of column absorber amounts (water vapor and ozone)
  2. Collection of surface reflectance. This activity inclues the measurement of a pre-selected areas and objects on the the ground
  3. Test flight of airborne or satellite-based sensor prototype or flight odel. This will require the installation of the sensor on the aicraft by DREAM/PHiLIDAR1
  4. Development of a radiative transfer code to predict at-sensor radiance. This will include the calculation of band-averaged over sensor spectral response and compare to reported radiance from sensor

Expected Output:

The expected outputs from the project are as follows:

  1. Calibration parameters for the remote sensing instrument
  2. Calibration method to be applied for the remote sensing instrument 3. Spectral signature database of key ground objects of signficance and/or importance that are especially endemic to the Philippine conditions
